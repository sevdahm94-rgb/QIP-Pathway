Inclusion Criteria

  • Aged 18 or over
  • Completed medically assisted alcohol withdrawal
  • Any gender with a mental health comorbidity
  • Abstinent from alcohol or mild lapse
  • Registered with an Ealing GP

Exclusion Criteria

  • Pregnant or breastfeeding
  • Renal impairment: Cr > 120µmol/L [acamprosate]; Severe impairment [naltrexone]
  • Hepatic impairment: Cautioned in severe hepatic failure [acamprosate]; Severe/acute impairment, acute hepatitis, or hepatic failure [naltrexone]
  • Active suicidal crisis requiring urgent secondary care
  • Severe psychiatric disorders requiring specialist care

Acamprosate: If using acamprosate, start treatment as soon as possible after assisted withdrawal.

Naltrexone: Offered as adjunct therapy in combination with an individual psychological intervention (cognitive behavioural therapies, behavioural therapies or social network and environment-based therapies) focused specifically on alcohol misuse.

Disulfiram* is only indicated after a successful withdrawal for people with moderate and severe alcohol dependence. Consider offering disulfiram in combination with a psychological intervention to service users who:

  • Have a goal of abstinence but for whom acamprosate and oral naltrexone are not suitable, OR
  • Prefer disulfiram and understand the relative risks of taking the drug.

*It will be reserved for selected, highly motivated patients and delivered via pharmacist-led supervised administration, aligning with NICE guidance that supervision should occur where possible.

Acamprosate

Service users should stay under pharmacist supervision, at least monthly, for 6 months, and at reduced but regular intervals if the drug is continued after 6 months.

Do not use blood tests routinely, but consider them to monitor for recovery of liver function and as a motivational aid for service users to show improvement.

Naltrexone

Service users should stay under pharmacist supervision, at least monthly, for 6 months, and at reduced but regular intervals if the drug is continued after 6 months.

Do not use blood tests routinely, but consider them for older people, for people with obesity, for monitoring recovery of liver function and as a motivational aid for service users to show improvement.

If the service user feels unwell advise them to stop oral naltrexone immediately.

Draw the service user's attention to the information card issued with oral naltrexone about its impact on opioid-based analgesics.

Disulfiram*

Make sure that service users taking disulfiram:

• stay under supervision, at least every 2 weeks for the first 2 months, then monthly for the following 4 months

• are medically monitored at least every 6 months after the initial 6 months of treatment and monitoring

Warn service users taking disulfiram, and their families and carers, about:

• the interaction between disulfiram and alcohol (which may also be found in food, perfume, aerosol sprays and so on), the symptoms of which may include flushing, nausea, palpitations and, more seriously, arrhythmias, hypotension and collapse

• the rapid and unpredictable onset of the rare complication of hepatotoxicity — advise service users that if they feel unwell or develop a fever or jaundice they should stop taking disulfiram and seek urgent medical attention

Reassess whether to:

Do NOT use antidepressants (including SSRIs) routinely for the treatment of alcohol misuse alone.
Do NOT use gammahydroxybutyrate (GHB) for the treatment of alcohol misuse.
Benzodiazepines: for withdrawal management only — NOT ongoing treatment for alcohol dependence.

Safety Netting & Self-Care Advice

When to seek urgent help

Advise service users to seek urgent medical attention if they experience any of the following:

• Symptoms of alcohol withdrawal (tremor, sweating, confusion, seizures) — even after successful assisted withdrawal

• Signs of severe liver problems: yellowing of skin or eyes, dark urine, persistent nausea or upper abdominal pain

Relapse prevention & triggers

Discuss with the service user:

• Identifying personal triggers for drinking (e.g. social situations, stress, emotions)

• Developing coping strategies and a relapse prevention plan

• Importance of avoiding alcohol completely while taking disulfiram — including in food, sauces, mouthwash, perfume and aerosol sprays

• If a lapse occurs: do not stop medication abruptly — contact the pharmacist or GP as soon as possible

Self-care & lifestyle advice (NICE CG115)

Encourage service users to:

• Engage with structured psychological support alongside pharmacological treatment (CBT, behavioural therapies or social network-based therapies)

• Maintain a balanced diet and stay well-hydrated — nutritional deficiencies are common in alcohol dependence

• Engage in regular physical activity to support mental wellbeing and reduce cravings

• Establish a daily routine to reduce unstructured time that may increase relapse risk

• Avoid people, places or situations closely associated with previous drinking behaviour where possible

Support networks & community resources

Signpost service users to:

• Alcoholics Anonymous (AA) — peer-led mutual aid and 12-step programme: www.alcoholics-anonymous.org.uk

• SMART Recovery — evidence-based self-management and recovery training programme

• Al-Anon — support for families and carers of people with alcohol dependence

• Local community alcohol services and drug and alcohol teams (DATs)

• NHS Talking Therapies (formerly IAPT) for psychological support
